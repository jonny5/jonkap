The Four Brahmaviharas 

Through the observation of spiders

One core teaching in Buddhism is around the Four Brahmaviharas (or divine abodes). These are ways of directing the heart in a loving way toward other beings (and ourselves). On a long retreat f

Metta (loving kindness)

I paced back and forth, slowly and diligently. While the intention is be attentive to the body and focus on each footstep, vision will often take dominance and something will catch my eye. On one such occassion, a black dot in the air took my attention. Seemingly falling like the world's smallest comet from the sky, the black dot grew and grew until landed on my shirt. 

The now larger volume began to crawl around, as it's spider nature became known to me. A strand of web was still attached, the only evidence that it had not actually flown through the sky. My immediate reaction was being startled. Once that settled, I felt a connection to this spider – as though she had sought me out. So with some care I lifted strand of web, spider attached, and placed her gently on a nearby bush.

Compassion (Love in the face of suffering)

After breakfast each morning, I would go to the walking meditation hall. It was usually empty (of humans), so I would start in the far corner of the room.

There was a spider who had remained dormant here for many days. A slight movement assured me it was still alive. I would check on it and it's total lack of stillness was a nice reassurance. My whole day had passed, my mind had gone through many states, my body would at times be very restless. And here was this spider, content in its stillness for that whole period.

One morning a woman entered the hall equipped with determination, vigor, and a rag. She began thoroughly cleaning the hall. I eyed her with some caution but without intervention. She made her way to that fateful corner. The rag met the spider. It's mostly still life became the permanent in death.

I teared up and laughed at my tears.

Joy (Resonant joy, joy in the face of another's joy)

A sprawling web's tension and strength bent all the branches of a bush to it's will, preserving its solid structure.  It's expansive volume turned graveyard for flies and bugs. Most unusually, the center of the web was covered in sticks and debris. I thought this peculiar and coincidental. I looked closer to find the puppeteer in the middle, hiding in it's midst. The clever spider lay ready and waiting, able to feel all the connection points of it's vast empire. The bounty of riches it had acquired as more than I could imagine a spider needing in a lifetime, so I nicknamed it Warren Buffett, the richest spider I had seen. Whenever I came to this walking path I saw the web standing strong as ever and smiled at the abundance of little Mr. Buffett. 

Equanimity (Steadiness)

At the end of the last sitting, I'd return each night to the my dormitory. Shrouded in the darkness, sometimes she would be there waiting, other times she was hidden away. Her web was often in tatters, destroyed by the stormy weather.

Yet when I left each morning, no matter what, the web had returned. It's intricate pattern never quite the same, yet the distinct work of a single resilient being.



All these relationships were one directional, I observed without interaction. The test of my feeling toward spiders would come later outside of a retreat setting. For several weeks, I'd notice a web covering my side mirror and I'd remove it for safety reasons. I wasn't driving often enough to think much of it. 

I headed off for a retreat in an isolated cabin, and when the web reappeared there I realized I had a spider inhabitant. I was kind of amazed that I had this companion for hours in the car and that I had taken her many miles from home. I hadn't ever noticed that the gap between the mirror and its housing were large enough for anything to make it through.I wasn't using the car for a week, so figured this might be her time to shine. She would notice the forest and without being interrupted could be free in a new locale. I removed the web for my drive home, hoping that it had been abandoned. Alas, it reappeared a few days later, it proved too nice of a home. 

For a while, I appreciated this companion, there was something reassuring about this spider always being there. I also felt a bit bad, whereas other spiders had their webs destroyed naturally, here I was destroying it nearly every day. 

One night I went out late into the evening, and finally saw her! She was starting the build her web, but very strategically as to not stray far from her escape route. I tried to safely remove her and just the air from my movement was enough to force a retreat.

I looked up strategies for my situation, which was more common than I realized. The most effective felt the cruelest: sealing the spider with a plastic bag to eventually suffocate it. I opted for a simpler approach of blowing some air into the gap between the mirror. I tried a few more gusts of air and left it that. We seemed to be in a stalemate and I thought I might be killing this spider slowly by virtue of needing to use my car. 

The air seemed to do have an effect. Because after that day I didn't see any webs in my side mirror. I had hoped she had found a new, more reliable home. When I saw a web appear on the car next to me in the parking lot, I realized I just pushed the problem to someone else.

The way that I was able to observe spiders during the retreat is akin to how things arise on the cushion of meditation. It's easiest to notice feelings when there isn't anything to immediately react to. And this is how metta practice is often done, we sit quietly and bring up loving kindness for an individual. One of my blockers for this *is* imagining the reality of the person and all the complexity in a relationship. I often use my brother's dog for Metta, and then my mind will wander: into the strange relationships between humans and dogs, love vs dependence, and on and on. The point of the practice though is to just focus on the *feeling* not the rational basis for it.

When action actually has to be taken is when feelings can become more complicated. One thing one does in metta practice when there is capacity is to think of an individual with whom you have a difficult relationship, and extending loving kindness to them. This is a very tangible way in which sitting on the cushion doing this is much easier than say, doing it when they are directly acting toward you in a way that is triggering. So in relationship and action is where one can see if the feelings can persist in face of adversity. And I did notice the gamut of emotions towards my spider companion. At first there was metta, then some compassion for continually destroying its web. Eventually frustration (I did not have the full capacity for equanimity).
