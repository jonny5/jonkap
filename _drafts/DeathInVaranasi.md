Death and Life in Varanasi

I walked with some conviction toward the burning ghat of Manikarnika. My gait did not prevent me from sticking out clearly as a tourist.  I was in Varanasi, and had learned in my time there that fading into obscurity here was not an option. A slight hesitation in my step was all the queue that was needed for a young man to grab my attention. The young man told me about his family's silks, and how I could check them out right by the ghat. I struggle with the balance of being kind and not wanting to either be prey to a scam or a potential waste of time as a customer. I assured him I was not interested in buying anything, and he responded that he would wait for for me to return to tell me more. I tried to kindly refuse and kept on my way.

Momentarily solo, I approached the large steps up to the ghat. I both wanted to observe the ritual and not commit any faux paux during it. The burning ghats are where in the Hindu religion bodies are ritually burned, and I now found myself in front of one of the largest and most sacred sites for doing so.

A man who looked somewhat official (and older) greeted me. Here, as an outsider, I was less resistant to deferring to a local. He explained how he was a guide for the ghat and gave me some history: the way the burning was performed and all the logistics involved. His emphasis was particularly on two aspects of the process: the special types of wood that are used and how many people waited nearby on death's door so their body would be treated properly when they passed.

We climbed the steps up the sturdy yet weathered structure, until we reached the center of the ritual. All around were platforms, fires coming from most. I observed the platforms, some had barely anything on them, others with ornately covered and wrapped bodies. The full covering provided some emotional distance. The arrival of a new body, ready for one of the empty platforms, caught my attention. I initially was focused on the logistics, the weight and work involved in transport. And then as it got closer the visceral nature of the proceedings were clear. The body was not fully visible, yet it was visibly human. Only a tattered sheet covered a the remains of the man, leaving parts of him exposed. 

The caste system was in full effect. The pyres of the ornately wrapped bodies were surrounded by many people (I imagine family a friends), this man was left to burn alone and with less care. The ceremony around each platform may have differed, the bodies burned all the same.

I sat there quietly, meditating. When smoke came toward me, I'd close my eyes until it passed, continuing to sit and reflect. I had wanted to sit there for a while and do a proper meditation. At times the smoke became very intense. I was willing to sit with it. My guide did not seem to feel the same, I could tell he was very ready to leave. So after a couple of minutes we made our way back down. 

The observation complete, I was ready to get on my way. While I had expected to compensate my guide for his time, I hadn't realized the extent of it. Rather than just asking me for a tip, he took me to a small street around the corner where his "boss" was waiting. The boss explained how, in order to support the proceedings they needed a LOT of money. I wasn't carrying much on me. I sensed something was off when I offered around 15 dollars and they were very upset. This wasn't like, "we appreciate your tip but would really like more", it was proper guilting and shaming. It did work on my slightly. I bumped up my tip to the equivalent of $20. They were angry it wasn't $200.

In order to avoid another similar interaction, I took a more circuitous route back around the ghat. The boy must have been keeping an eye on my movements. Because when I emerged from a dense tangle of streets back toward the ghat, there he was. Waiting for me.

Maransati and Death Contemplation

My intention in visiting the ghat was to practice with the Buddhist idea of Maranasati (contemplation of death). The Buddha gives explicit ways of practicing this, specifically on meditating with bodies in various states of decomposition. I hadn't practiced with this explicitly before, mostly I was familiar with the Five Rememberances:

1. I am of the nature to age, I cannot avoid aging

2. I am of the nature to get sick, I cannot avoid sickness

3. I am of the nature to die, I cannot avoid death

4. Everything I hold dear will change, I will be separated from all I love

5. I am the owner of my actions, heir to my actions

Contemplating in this way was the intention of my visit. My only experience with dead bodies had been those belonging to family members. For understandable reasons in those moments I was not in an emotional headspace to reflect on the nature of death and the body in a wise way.

For a few moments sitting with the burning body, there was profundity. Looking at the man's body burn, his hair and parts of his flesh visible as the fire was lit under him still sticks with me, the clear contrast of his end compared to those other platforms. Some sadness came through, as did some peace. There was value in seeing the body as just a body. There was the shared humanity of knowing this body once lived a full life and also the distance of having no details or relationship to that life. 

If I am being honest, the experience was short lived. The smoke became overwhelming, my guide was impatient. I did not get sit deeply in contemplation of the Buddha's teachings on the body and it's impermanence. 

What immediately followed provoked a stronger reaction. While I intended to get a specific lesson on death, instead the impermanence of all experience. The time spent in the ghat was matched by the time spent arguing with my guide and his boss. I learned back at my hostel that the guide was running a definite scam, that no money he was collecting was going toward the ritual or to support the funeral rites of men such as the one I observed. At the time I wasn't totally sure, but their approach to asking me for money felt very disconnected from the ritual so it did make sense.

What was being performed as a sacred ritual was surrounded by the desires of living humans. Me, a tourist, attempting to use the ritual to understand myself. And the guide, using it as a source of income. 

Capping off the experience with the boy waiting for me only further solidified my self perception as a walking dollar sign. I thought maybe my lack of sternness in ensuring him I didn't want to buy anything was not enough so he still saw me as his best shot. My frustration mixed with my attempts at friendliness (and conflict aversion) again. 

A good lesson that setting out with an intention to learn something can only go so far. While I had wanted to get a specific learning from the dead, it was the living who could teach me most about my own limitations.
